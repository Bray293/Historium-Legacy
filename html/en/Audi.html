AUDI

The beginnings of Audi
(1900s-1930s)

The story of Audi begins in 1909 when August Horch, a German automotive engineer, founded the Audi company after leaving his first enterprise, Horch. The name "Audi" is the Latin translation of "Horch," which means "listen" in German.

Audi's first car, the Audi Type A, was produced in 1910. From the very beginning, Audi distinguished itself with technical innovations and a strong commitment to motorsport, with victories in the famous Austrian mountain race.

The Interwar Period
(1930s-1940s)

In 1932, Audi merged with three other German car manufacturers: Horch, DKW, and Wanderer to form Auto Union, symbolized by the four rings of Audi's logo. Auto Union became a major player in luxury cars and motorsports, notably with the famous "Silver Arrows" that dominated Grand Prix competitions.

Despite its success on the racetrack, World War II interrupted Audi's activities, and Auto Union's factories were heavily damaged.

The Rebirth of Audi
(1960s-1970s)

After the war, Auto Union was re-established in West Germany, but it wasn't until 1965, after Volkswagen's acquisition, that the Audi brand truly revived. The introduction of the Audi 100 in 1968 marked the beginning of a new era, with a focus on performance, quality, and technological innovation.

Audi's lineup continued to grow with models like the Audi 80, establishing Audi as a premium car brand.

The 1980s
Quattro and the All-Wheel Drive Era

The 1980s were a pivotal period for Audi with the launch of the Audi Quattro in 1980, the first sports car with all-wheel drive. This technology revolutionized rally racing and allowed Audi to dominate competitions for several years.

This decade also saw Audi strengthen its image as an innovative manufacturer with advances in engine technology, aerodynamics, and design.

Global Expansion and Diversification
(1990s-2000s)

In the 1990s, Audi established itself as a global brand with models such as the Audi A4 (1994) and the Audi A8 (1994), a luxury sedan equipped with Quattro technology. The introduction of the Audi TT (1998) also demonstrated the brand’s ability to combine bold design and performance.

The early 2000s saw Audi diversify further into the SUV segment with the Audi Q7 (2005) and sports models like the Audi R8 (2006), an iconic supercar.

The Technological Turn
(2010s)

The 2010s marked a technological shift for Audi, with a focus on hybrid and electric vehicles. The Audi e-tron, launched in 2018, was the brand’s first fully electric car, symbolizing its commitment to electromobility.

At the same time, Audi continued to innovate with its traditional models like the Audi A6, A7, and A8, incorporating advanced technologies such as semi-autonomous driving and state-of-the-art infotainment systems.

Recent Models
(2020 and beyond)

Today, Audi continues to expand its portfolio of electric and hybrid vehicles. The Audi Q4 e-tron, launched in 2021, marks a new stage in the brand's electrification strategy, with a compact SUV designed to meet modern consumers' expectations for sustainability and performance.

Audi has set ambitious goals for the future, including reducing its carbon footprint and continuously improving its vehicles in terms of technology and comfort.
