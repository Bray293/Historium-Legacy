MERCEDES-BENZ

The beginnings of Mercedes-Benz
(1886-1920)

Mercedes-Benz traces its roots to the revolutionary inventions of Karl Benz and Gottlieb Daimler. In 1886, Karl Benz invented what is often considered the world's first car, the Benz Patent Motorwagen. At the same time, Daimler developed his own internal combustion engine.

In 1926, Benz's and Daimler's companies merged to form Daimler-Benz, giving birth to the Mercedes-Benz brand, named after the daughter of one of Daimler's associates, Emil Jellinek.

The Interwar Years
(1920-1940)

Between the two world wars, Mercedes-Benz became a benchmark in the automotive industry with luxurious and technologically advanced vehicles. One of their first great innovations was the Mercedes-Benz SSK, an iconic sports car designed by Ferdinand Porsche.

The brand also began to diversify, producing commercial vehicles and trucks.

Post-war recovery and revival
(1940-1960)

After World War II, Mercedes-Benz rebuilt its automotive empire by focusing on high-quality engineering. In 1954, the legendary Mercedes 300SL Gullwing was launched, featuring its famous gullwing doors and a direct fuel injection engine, marking a turning point in the history of sports cars.

During this period, the brand also emphasized luxury, safety, and performance, qualities that would define Mercedes-Benz for decades to come.

Technological Innovations
(1960-1980)

In the 1960s, Mercedes-Benz innovated by introducing safety systems such as the safety cell and crumple zones to better protect passengers in case of an accident. The Mercedes-Benz S-Class, launched in 1972, became the brand's showcase for new technologies, comfort, and safety innovations.

The 1980s were marked by the introduction of ABS (Anti-lock Braking System) technology on several models, setting a new standard for automotive safety.

Expansion and Globalization
(1980-2000)

The 1980s and 1990s saw Mercedes-Benz expand globally, strengthening its product range to include not only luxury sedans and limousines but also SUVs, coupes, and commercial vehicles. The E-Class, launched in 1993, became one of the brand's most popular models.

In 1998, Mercedes-Benz merged with the American manufacturer Chrysler, forming DaimlerChrysler. Although this merger was dissolved in 2007, it marked a turning point in the internationalization of Mercedes-Benz.

The Modern Era and Innovation
(2000-2020)

From the 2000s, Mercedes-Benz focused on innovation, electrification, and connectivity. In 2010, the Mercedes-Benz SLS AMG marked the return of gullwing doors, with a futuristic design and stunning performance.

The brand also introduced its first hybrid and electric cars, with the EQ lineup symbolizing Mercedes-Benz's commitment to a more sustainable future.

The Future of Mercedes-Benz
(2020 and beyond)

In 2020, Mercedes-Benz introduced the EQS, a fully electric luxury sedan and the first of the EQ range to use a dedicated electric vehicle architecture. Mercedes-Benz continues to innovate with autonomous driving systems, advanced connectivity, and a commitment to sustainability.

The brand aims to achieve carbon neutrality by 2039, with 100% electric models such as the EQA, EQC, and EQS marking this transition toward a cleaner future.
