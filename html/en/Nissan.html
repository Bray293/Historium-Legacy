NISSAN

The Beginnings of Nissan
(1910s-1930s)

The history of Nissan dates back to 1911, when Masujiro Hashimoto founded the Kwaishinsha Motor Car Works in Tokyo, Japan. It was in 1914 that the company produced its first car, named DAT (an acronym for Den, Aoyama, and Takeuchi, the three main investors). This car was a compact model designed to meet the growing demand for vehicles in Japan.

In 1931, a smaller version of the DAT car was released under the name DATSON, literally "the son of DAT." However, the name was quickly changed to Datsun in 1933, as "son" in Japanese also means "loss." That year, Nissan Motor Co., Ltd. was officially created.

Nissan During and After World War II
(1940s-1950s)

During World War II, Nissan diverted part of its production to meet the military needs of Japan. After the war, Nissan rebuilt itself and resumed the production of civilian cars.

In the 1950s, Nissan collaborated with Austin Motor Company in the UK to produce vehicles under license, which helped the brand improve its manufacturing techniques and modernize.

One of the first cars from this era was the Nissan Patrol (1951), a rugged all-terrain vehicle that marked Nissan's entry into 4x4s.

Global Expansion
(1960s-1970s)

In the 1960s, Nissan began exporting its cars massively internationally, notably to the United States, under the Datsun brand. In 1966, Nissan merged with Prince Motor Company, adding popular models like the Skyline to its portfolio.

One of the standout models from this period is the Datsun 240Z (1969), a revolutionary sports car that achieved immense success in the United States and beyond for its combination of performance and affordability. This model established Nissan's reputation in sports cars.

In 1973, the Datsun Cherry was launched, becoming one of the first Nissan cars to be sold en masse internationally.

The 1980s
The Transition from Datsun to Nissan

The 1980s were marked by a rebranding of the company. Nissan decided to gradually phase out the Datsun name in favor of the Nissan brand globally. It was in 1983 that Datsun officially disappeared from the market.

During this period, Nissan produced some of the most iconic models in its history:

  • Nissan 300ZX (1983), a sports car that continued the Z lineage and was famous for its performance.
  • The Nissan Maxima (1981), a luxury sedan.
  • The Nissan Pulsar (1982), which helped the brand compete in the small car segment.

The 1990s
Innovation and Challenges

The 1990s were marked by the rise of new competitors and an economic recession in Japan, but Nissan continued its technical innovations. Among the famous models:

  • The Nissan Skyline GT-R (R32, R33, R34) which, thanks to its advanced technologies and powerful RB26DETT engine, acquired cult status among sports car enthusiasts. The GT-R was particularly famous for its success in races and its appearances in popular culture, notably in movies and video games.
  • The Nissan Altima (1992), which became a very popular sedan in North America.

The Alliance with Renault
(2000s)

In 1999, Nissan experienced severe financial difficulties and formed a strategic alliance with Renault. Carlos Ghosn, then CEO of Renault, took charge of Nissan and launched a recovery plan that brought Nissan back to profitability.

It was during this period that Nissan introduced several new models:

  • Nissan X-Trail (2000), a compact SUV.
  • Nissan 350Z (2002), which revived the Z range with a modern sports model.
  • Nissan Murano (2002), a crossover SUV that gained popularity for its bold design.

The 2010s
Electrification and Innovation

In the 2010s, Nissan positioned itself as a pioneer in electric mobility with the launch of the Nissan Leaf (2010), the first electric vehicle designed for mass consumption. The Leaf has sold hundreds of thousands of units worldwide, establishing Nissan as a leader in the electric vehicle market.

The company also launched several other innovative models, such as the Nissan Qashqai (2006), which contributed to the growing popularity of compact SUVs.

Toward a Sustainable Future
(2020s and Beyond)

In the 2020s, Nissan continues its commitment to sustainability and innovation. The company has set ambitious goals for reducing its carbon footprint and aims to electrify its range significantly in the coming years.

With the launch of new models, such as the Nissan Ariya (2020), an all-electric crossover, Nissan is working to remain at the forefront of the automotive industry while addressing environmental challenges.
