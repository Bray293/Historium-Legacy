FORD

The Beginnings of Ford
(1900s-1920s)

The story of Ford begins in 1903 when Henry Ford founded the Ford Motor Company in Dearborn, Michigan. In 1908, Ford revolutionized the automotive industry with the creation of the famous **Model T**, the first mass-produced car at an affordable price thanks to the assembly line.

The **Model T** transformed American society, making cars accessible to the middle class, and in just 19 years, over 15 million units were produced.

The Expansion Years
(1930s-1940s)

In the 1930s, Ford continued its expansion with new innovations, including the **Ford V8** in 1932, a car that democratized V8 engines for the public. Despite the Great Depression, Ford maintained its industrial leadership.

During World War II, Ford temporarily halted civilian car production to focus on war efforts, manufacturing military vehicles and equipment for the Allies.

Post-War Boom and the 1950s
(1950s-1960s)

After World War II, Ford focused on creating more modern and stylish cars. The **Ford Thunderbird** (1955), an iconic car of this period, embodied elegance and driving pleasure. This model was a huge success in the United States.

Ford also introduced advanced technologies, such as power steering and hydraulic brakes, which became increasingly common in its cars.

The 1960s
The Mustang Era

The 1960s were marked by the launch of one of the most famous cars in automotive history: the **Ford Mustang** (1964). This affordable sports coupe quickly became a symbol of American car culture, especially for younger generations.

The Mustang was an incredible success, becoming a cult model in the industry and a benchmark for muscle cars.

The 1970s and 1980s
Pickups and Diversification

In the 1970s, Ford strengthened its position with the **Ford F-150**, a rugged pickup truck that quickly became a best-seller in North America. To this day, Ford's F-Series remains the top-selling vehicle lineup in the United States.

In the 1980s, Ford further diversified with the introduction of more compact cars, such as the **Ford Escort** (1980) and the **Ford Sierra** (1982) in Europe, which catered to the growing demand for fuel-efficient vehicles.

The 1990s
Globalization and Innovation

In the 1990s, Ford adopted a global strategy, expanding its international operations. The brand introduced innovative models like the **Ford Mondeo** (1993), which became highly successful in Europe.

During this period, Ford also focused on technological innovations, making advancements in safety, fuel efficiency, and the introduction of new onboard technologies.

The 2000s
Revival with Supercars and SUVs

The 2000s marked Ford's revival with the return of supercars like the **Ford GT** (2005), a modern homage to the famous Ford GT40, which won the 24 Hours of Le Mans in the 1960s.

Ford continued to innovate in the SUV segment with models like the **Ford Explorer** and **Ford Escape**, catering to new market trends.

The 2010s
Electrification and Sustainable Mobility

In the 2010s, Ford began electrifying its lineup with hybrid and electric models, such as the **Ford Focus Electric** and **Ford Fusion Hybrid**. Ford invested heavily in research and development to meet the growing demand for sustainable mobility.

In 2020, Ford unveiled its all-electric SUV, the **Ford Mustang Mach-E**, a model that combines sportiness with eco-responsibility.

Ford's Future
Towards Future Mobility

Ford continues to focus on the future with investments in autonomous driving technologies, vehicle connectivity, and electrification. The company aims for a fully electrified lineup by 2030.

With vehicles like the **Ford F-150 Lightning** (electric) and initiatives in shared mobility, Ford is determined to play a leading role in the transformation of the automotive industry.
